# Chinese Labs Flood the Zone While DeepSeek R1 Keeps Climbing

**Week of 2026-03-02 to 2026-03-06**

This was the week Chinese AI labs made their ambitions impossible to ignore. Two major model families dropped within days of each other, DeepSeek R1 continued its relentless climb up the download charts, and the sheer breadth of releases — from MoE giants to TTS systems — signaled a strategic shift from catching up to setting the pace.

---

## 1. Qwen3.5 Drops a Full Arsenal: MoE, Coding, Vision, and Voice

Alibaba's Qwen team didn't release a model this week — they released an entire ecosystem. **Qwen3.5** arrived as a coordinated family: a 397B-parameter MoE flagship (with only 17B active per forward pass), a compact 35B-A3B variant for edge deployment, a coding-specialized Coder-Next, and a text-to-speech model. NVIDIA immediately followed with an FP4-quantized version of the 397B, and Unsloth shipped GGUFs within hours. (1,152 likes | 1.03M downloads across variants)

The strategy here is unmistakable. While Western labs focus on shipping one frontier model at a time, Qwen is competing across every modality simultaneously — chat, code, vision, and now voice. The 35B-A3B variant deserves special attention: at 3B active parameters, it's designed to run on consumer hardware while maintaining multimodal capabilities including image understanding. (846 likes | 680.5K downloads for the 35B alone)

**Why it matters:** The MoE architecture is becoming the default for efficiency-conscious deployment. A 397B model that only activates 17B parameters per token gives you frontier-class capability at a fraction of the inference cost. For builders choosing between API providers and self-hosting, Qwen3.5 just made self-hosting dramatically more attractive.

**What's next:** Watch for benchmark comparisons against Llama 4 and Mistral Large. The real test is whether the MoE routing holds up on diverse real-world tasks, not just curated evals.

## 2. Kimi-K2.5: Moonshot AI's Multimodal Dark Horse

**Moonshot AI** quietly became one of the most downloaded model providers on HuggingFace this week. Kimi-K2.5, their latest multimodal model, racked up 1.71 million downloads and 2,200 likes — numbers that put it ahead of most Western open-weight releases. (2,200 likes | 1.71M downloads)

Kimi-K2.5 handles both text and vision tasks, and early community benchmarks suggest it punches well above its weight class on Chinese-language understanding and visual reasoning. Moonshot has been building steadily — their Kimi chatbot already has significant consumer traction in China — but this is their strongest play yet for the global developer community.

**Why it matters:** The download numbers tell the real story. Developers aren't just curious — they're integrating. Moonshot is carving out a niche as the "third option" among Chinese AI labs, distinct from both Qwen's breadth-first approach and DeepSeek's reasoning focus. For teams building multilingual applications, Kimi-K2.5 is now a serious contender.

**What's next:** Moonshot's challenge is ecosystem, not capability. They need the fine-tuning tooling, quantization support, and community momentum that Qwen and DeepSeek already have. The download surge suggests they're getting there.

[Read more ->](https://huggingface.co/moonshotai/Kimi-K2.5)

## 3. DeepSeek R1 Won't Stop Growing

**DeepSeek-R1** continues its remarkable run as the most-liked model on HuggingFace — 13,096 likes and counting, with nearly a million downloads this week alone. No new version dropped; this is pure sustained adoption momentum. (13,096 likes | 928.3K downloads)

The reasoning model that shook the industry earlier this year hasn't faded into the background the way most open-weight releases do. Developers keep finding new use cases: chain-of-thought pipelines, agentic reasoning loops, and complex multi-step problem solving where R1's explicit reasoning traces provide both better outputs and built-in interpretability.

**Why it matters:** R1's staying power validates the reasoning-model paradigm. This isn't a hype cycle — it's a fundamental shift in how developers build AI systems. The fact that R1 is still accumulating downloads at this rate, months after release, suggests it's becoming infrastructure rather than just another model option.

**What's next:** DeepSeek has been unusually quiet about R2. The longer they wait, the higher expectations climb. Meanwhile, R1's community of fine-tuners and distillers keeps growing, making the R1 architecture a de facto standard for open reasoning.

[Read more ->](https://huggingface.co/deepseek-ai/DeepSeek-R1)

## 4. The Real Story: China's AI Ecosystem Is Self-Sustaining

Step back from the individual releases and the pattern is striking. Three of this week's top five stories come from Chinese labs — and they're not copying Western architectures anymore. Qwen is pioneering efficient MoE deployment across modalities. Moonshot is building multimodal models with distinct strengths. DeepSeek defined the reasoning-model category that everyone else is now chasing.

A year ago, the narrative was "Chinese labs are 6-12 months behind." That framing is dead. These teams are shipping original architectures, building their own developer ecosystems, and competing on download counts with anyone. The open-weight ecosystem is now genuinely multipolar.

**Why it matters:** For builders, this means more options, more competition on quality, and less dependence on any single provider or geography. For the industry, it means the innovation frontier is wider than Silicon Valley. The best model for your use case might come from Hangzhou, Beijing, or Shanghai — and increasingly, it's open-weight.

**What's next:** The frontier lab consolidation that many predicted hasn't happened. Instead, we're seeing specialization: DeepSeek on reasoning, Qwen on breadth, Moonshot on multimodal consumer applications. Expect this fragmentation to accelerate, not reverse.

## 5. TensorFlow Trends Again — But Why Now?

In a week dominated by new model releases, **TensorFlow** trending on GitHub feels almost nostalgic. Google's venerable ML framework surged back into visibility with 193K+ stars, driven by renewed interest from researchers and enterprise teams who never left the ecosystem. (193,955 stars | 75,215 forks)

The timing isn't random. As model deployment becomes the bottleneck — not model training — TensorFlow's mature serving infrastructure (TF Serving, TF Lite, TFX pipelines) is looking increasingly attractive. PyTorch won the research war, but production deployment is a different game.

**Why it matters:** The framework wars are over, but the deployment wars are just starting. Teams running open-weight models like Qwen3.5 and DeepSeek-R1 in production need battle-tested infrastructure. TensorFlow's resurgence reflects a pragmatic industry shift from "what's the best model" to "how do I actually ship this."

**What's next:** Google's been quietly investing in TensorFlow-JAX interoperability. If they nail the bridge between research flexibility (JAX) and production reliability (TF Serving), they could reclaim significant developer mindshare.
